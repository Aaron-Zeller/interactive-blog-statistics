The Decision Point: Statistical Inference in Action

Descriptive analysis tells you what happened in the sample. Inference asks whether the pattern is strong enough to support a decision beyond that one sample.

What You Will Learn In This Section

  • How expected lift, observed gaps, and confidence intervals fit together in an A/B test.
  • What a z-statistic and a p-value are measuring under the null model.
  • Why repeated sampling can make a seemingly strong result look much less stable.

Scene 1

First concept: what an A/B test actually is.

An A/B test compares two versions under controlled conditions. Group A sees the current version, Group B sees the change, and we compare outcomes using the same metric and the same measurement window. Random assignment matters because it makes the groups comparable before outcomes are observed.

Randomization is not flashy, but it is what lets differences be interpreted as evidence rather than coincidence or selection bias.

Second concept: expected lift is your pre-data hypothesis.

Before collecting data, you should say what improvement would be practically meaningful. For conversion rates, lift is usually expressed in percentage points, not relative percent change.

Try it: drag the green distribution until the distance feels practically meaningful, not just barely visible. This is the moment to define what counts as an effect worth caring about before the data arrives.

Scene 4

Fourth concept: start with the thing we care about, the mean gap.

Every test starts with a simple comparison. For conversion, that comparison is a difference in rates. For numeric outcomes, it is a difference in means. This raw difference is the effect we are trying to explain.

Try it: drag the green curve and watch the raw gap change before any standardization happens. This scene is here to separate the effect itself from the later question of whether it is large relative to noise.

The raw gap is the signal of interest. On its own, however, it does not say whether the difference is large relative to the noise in the measurement process.

Scene 5

Fifth concept: standardize the gap into a z-statistic.

A raw gap becomes much more interpretable once we divide it by its standard error. The result is a standardized statistic that tells us how far the observed effect is from zero in units of estimated noise.

Try it: change the gap and the standard error separately. The key habit here is to distinguish a large raw effect from a large standardized effect.

Move the gap or SE slider and watch where the resulting z lands on the normal reference curve. The orange marker is your observed z-statistic, not a probability region yet.

z = (gap - 0) / SE, measured in standard errors from no effect.

Once we have a z-statistic, we ask how unusual it would be if the true effect were really zero. The p-value answers exactly that question and nothing more.

The Assumption Crash: When Models Stop Matching Reality

You ship the product, congratulate yourself for being data-driven, and then the sales numbers collapse catastrophically. A correct formula can still produce a bad decision if the assumptions behind it are wrong. This act is about checking those assumptions before trusting the result.

What You Will Learn In This Section

  • Why statistical tests are only trustworthy when their assumptions are reasonable.
  • How histograms and QQ plots help diagnose whether a reference model fits the data.
  • Why poor model fit can inflate false positives and lead to costly decisions.

Scene 1

You shipped the winner. The revenue chart fell off a cliff.

Your supervisor had the celebratory slide ready. The stakeholders were ready to brag. Then the live numbers arrived, and they were dreadful. The formula may be mathematically correct, but the world may not match it. If observations are dependent, segments behave differently, or the process is unstable, then the p-value can look tidy while the decision is not.

Scene 2

Stable Process

Did traffic mix, seasonality, or tracking definitions change during the experiment window?

Model Match

Does the test family match the metric type, or are we forcing the wrong distributional story?

Scale Of Noise

Are hidden shocks and overdispersion making your standard errors too optimistic?

The Estimation Step: Point Estimates Before “Max It”

Once you think you know which variable matters, the temptation is to turn it up as far as possible. Point estimation is the step where you slow down and quantify how large the effect appears to be in the data.

What You Will Learn In This Section

  • What a point estimate is and why it is only one plausible value from one sample.
  • How repeated samples create a distribution of estimates around the true parameter.
  • How Method of Moments and Maximum Likelihood approach estimation differently.

Scene 1

The manager plan: push one lever to 11.

“If higher promo score is associated with higher revenue, just maximize promo score” sounds decisive. It is also not yet analysis. Before acting, we need a point estimate: one best numerical summary of the effect in the sample we actually observed.

Scene 2

Point estimate = one best guess from one sample.

A point estimate is a single value computed from the sample. In a simple linear model, the slope estimate tells us how much the outcome changes, on average, when the predictor increases by one unit.

Estimated slope

$$\hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum_i (x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})}{\sum_i (x_i-\bar{x})^2}$$

Estimated line

$$\hat{y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 x$$

Predicted change

$$\widehat{\Delta y} = \hat{\beta}_1 \cdot \Delta x$$

Scene 5

Method of Moments: match what your sample “looks like.”

A moment is a numerical summary built from powers of the data. The first moment captures location, and the second central moment captures spread. Method of Moments chooses parameters so the model reproduces those same summaries.

In this example, MoM is straightforward: match the sample’s center and spread to the center and spread implied by the model. It is fast and interpretable, but it can react strongly when a few extreme values distort the sample moments.

Moment Matching (Laplace)

$$\hat{\mu}_{\text{MoM}}=\bar{x},\qquad \hat{b}_{\text{MoM}}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{2n}\sum_i (x_i-\bar{x})^2}$$

MoM Intuition

$$\text{sample location + spread} \approx \text{model location + spread}$$

Scene 6

Maximum Likelihood: choose parameters that make this sample most plausible.

Maximum likelihood estimation asks which parameter values make the observed sample most plausible under the model. Instead of matching a few summaries, it uses the full likelihood of the observed data.

For Laplace data, this leads to a center estimate based on the median rather than the mean. That makes the location estimate less sensitive to outliers. MoM says “match summaries”; MLE says “maximize plausibility.”

Likelihood Idea

$$\hat{\theta}_{\text{MLE}}=\arg\max_{\theta}\prod_{i=1}^n f(x_i\mid\theta)$$

Closed Form (Laplace)

$$\hat{\mu}_{\text{MLE}}=\mathrm{median}(x_i),\qquad \hat{b}_{\text{MLE}}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_i \lvert x_i-\hat{\mu}_{\text{MLE}}\rvert$$

The Public Claim: Formal Testing Before Public Bragging

Estimation tells you what the sample suggests. Formal testing tells you how to express that evidence in a disciplined way when the claim will be presented as more than an internal hunch.

What You Will Learn In This Section

  • How hypothesis testing differs from parameter estimation and what the core testing terms mean.
  • When to use z-tests, t-tests, paired tests, and two-sample tests.
  • How p-values, power, sidedness, and multiple testing shape formal statistical conclusions.

Scene 1

Parameter estimation vs statistical testing.

Estimation and testing are related, but they answer different questions. Estimation focuses on magnitude: how large is the effect? Testing focuses on evidence against a specific null claim.

Estimation

$$\text{Estimate } \theta \text{ and quantify uncertainty around } \hat{\theta}$$

Testing

$$H_0:\theta=\theta_0 \quad\text{vs}\quad H_1:\theta\neq\theta_0$$

Scene 2

Hypothesis-testing dictionary

Statistical testing has a small vocabulary that needs to stay precise. Getting these terms straight makes the later procedures much easier to follow.

Null Hypothesis

\(H_0\): the reference claim you test against, usually “no effect” or “no difference.”

$$H_0:\theta=\theta_0$$

Alternative Hypothesis

\(H_1\): the competing claim you would support if the data looks inconsistent with \(H_0\).

$$H_1:\theta\neq\theta_0\ \text{or}\ \theta>\theta_0\ \text{or}\ \theta<\theta_0$$

Significance Level

\(\alpha\): the false-positive tolerance you choose before looking at the result.

$$\alpha=P(\text{Reject }H_0\mid H_0\text{ true})$$

Test Statistic

A number computed from the sample that measures how far the observed estimate is from the null value.

$$T=\frac{\text{estimate}-\text{null value}}{\text{standard error}}$$

p-value

The probability, assuming \(H_0\), of seeing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one you observed.

Decision Rule

If \(p\le\alpha\), reject \(H_0\). If \(p>\alpha\), fail to reject \(H_0\). That is not proof that \(H_0\) is true; it only means the sample was not convincing enough.

Practical translation: a small p-value means “hard to explain by random variation if the null model were true,” not “guaranteed success.”

Scene 3

How to perform a statistical test, step by step.

A statistical test is not a single button press. It is a short sequence of design and interpretation choices, and skipping any one of them weakens the conclusion.

Step 1: Frame The Question

State the metric, the effect size that would matter in practice, and the hypotheses (\(H_0, H_1\)).

Step 2: Pick The Test

Choose a test whose assumptions fit the data and the question you are asking.

Step 3: Lock Rules Before Data Peek

Set \(\alpha\), choose one-sided or two-sided testing, and decide on any multiple-testing correction in advance.

Step 4: Compute Statistic

Use the sample to compute the estimate, its standard error, and the resulting test statistic.

Step 5: Quantify Evidence

Turn the test statistic into a p-value or compare it with a pre-defined critical threshold.

Step 6: Report Responsibly

Report the decision together with the estimated effect and uncertainty, not the p-value alone.

Suggested reporting template: “Estimated lift = X, 95% CI = [L, U], test = ..., p = ..., assumptions checked = ...”.

Scene 4

Z-test concept: what it tests, why it works.

A z-test asks whether the observed sample mean is farther from a null mean than we would expect from random sampling alone. It relies on a normal model and a known population standard deviation \(\sigma\).

Under \(H_0:\mu=\mu_0\), the sampling distribution of \(\bar{X}\) is centered at \(\mu_0\) with standard error \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\). So the natural next step is to measure the observed mean in standard-error units:

Z Statistic

$$Z=\frac{\bar{X}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}$$

Null Distribution

$$Z\mid H_0 \sim \mathcal N(0,1)$$

If \(H_0\) is true, large values of \(|Z|\) are rare. That is why a large standardized gap translates into a small p-value.

Scene 6

t-test concept: what changes when variance is unknown.

In practice, the population spread \(\sigma\) is usually unknown. We replace it with the sample standard deviation \(s\), which introduces extra uncertainty because the denominator is now estimated from the same data.

The correct null reference becomes Student’s t distribution with \(n-1\) degrees of freedom. Its heavier tails acknowledge that extra uncertainty and therefore make the test more conservative for smaller samples.

t Statistic

$$T=\frac{\bar{X}-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}}$$

Null Distribution

$$T\mid H_0 \sim t_{n-1}$$

As the sample size grows, the estimate of spread becomes more stable and the t distribution approaches the normal distribution. That is why z-tests and t-tests become very similar at large \(n\).

Scene 8

Two-sample t-test: comparing two independent groups.

Sometimes you are not comparing one sample against a fixed benchmark. You are comparing two separate groups: control versus treatment, old layout versus new layout, one region versus another. The quantity of interest is then the difference between two sample means.

The key assumption is independence between the two groups. If the users in group A are not the same users as the users in group B, then each group contributes its own mean and its own uncertainty. The standard error has to account for both sources of sampling noise.

Difference In Means

$$\hat{\Delta}=\bar{X}_1-\bar{X}_2$$

Welch Two-Sample t

$$T=\frac{\bar{X}_1-\bar{X}_2}{\sqrt{s_1^2/n_1+s_2^2/n_2}}$$

In practice, Welch's t-test is usually the safer default because it does not require the two groups to have exactly the same variance. If the groups are independent, this is the right family of test. If the same people appear in both groups, it is not.

Scene 9

Paired t-test: test the within-person change, not the raw scores.

A paired design happens when observations come in natural pairs: before and after for the same user, left hand versus right hand for the same person, or matched pairs chosen to be directly comparable. In that setup, the data point is really the difference inside each pair.

That changes the analysis completely. Rather than comparing two independent means, we compute one difference per pair and run a one-sample t-test on those differences. This often gives a cleaner estimate because stable person-to-person variation cancels out.

Pairwise Differences

$$d_i = x_{i,\mathrm{after}} - x_{i,\mathrm{before}}$$

Paired t Statistic

$$T=\frac{\bar{d}-\mu_{d,0}}{s_d/\sqrt{n}}$$

The paired t-test is powerful when pairing is meaningful, because each person acts as their own reference. If you ignore the pairing and run an independent-groups test instead, you throw away that structure and can make the result noisier than it needs to be.

Scene 10

Type I and Type II errors.

Every test balances two kinds of mistakes. A Type I error means declaring an effect that is not really there. A Type II error means missing a real effect. Reducing one often makes the other more likely unless you improve the design with more data or a stronger signal.

Type I Error

$$\alpha=P(\text{Reject }H_0\mid H_0\text{ true})$$

Type II Error

$$\beta=P(\text{Fail to reject }H_0\mid H_1\text{ true})$$

Power

$$1-\beta=P(\text{Reject }H_0\mid H_1\text{ true})$$

Scene 11

Effect Size: how big is the effect, really?

Effect size is about magnitude, not merely detectability. It answers “how large is the difference?” rather than only “did the test cross a threshold?”

Lift is related but not identical. Lift is usually the business-facing change in raw or relative units, whereas effect size is the statistical measure of magnitude, often standardized to make comparisons across settings easier.

Absolute Lift

$$\text{Lift}_{\text{abs}}=\bar{y}_{\text{treat}}-\bar{y}_{\text{control}}$$

Unit: same as metric (e.g., dollars, percentage points).

Relative Lift

$$\text{Lift}_{\text{rel}}=\frac{\bar{y}_{\text{treat}}-\bar{y}_{\text{control}}}{\bar{y}_{\text{control}}}$$

Unitless ratio, but tied to baseline level.

Standardized Effect Size

$$d=\frac{\bar{y}_{\text{treat}}-\bar{y}_{\text{control}}}{s_{\text{pooled}}}$$

Unitless “how many SDs apart” magnitude (useful across metrics).

In practice, report both: effect size for statistical magnitude and lift for business interpretation.

Scene 15

Multiple testing: false discoveries scale fast.

When many hypotheses are tested at once, chance alone will usually make some of them look significant. If all nulls are true and each test uses level \(\alpha\), the expected number of false positives is \(m\alpha\), and the probability of at least one false positive increases quickly with \(m\).

What To Remember From This Act

  • Hypothesis testing is about evidence against a null claim, not proof that a result is important.
  • The right test depends on the design, the unknowns, and whether observations are independent or paired.
  • P-values, power, sidedness, and multiple testing all shape what counts as a responsible public claim.

Wrap-Up

Thanks for working through the blog

I hope this explorable helped make the path from raw data to statistical judgment a bit clearer. The goal was not just to find effects, but to build better habits for reading evidence carefully.

Five ideas worth keeping with you

  • Start with data quality. A polished analysis cannot rescue missing, irrelevant, or inappropriate variables.
  • Plot before you summarize. A visual can reveal skew, clusters, outliers, or strange geometry that summary statistics hide.
  • Check assumptions before trusting a test result. A small p-value is only meaningful if the model behind it is still plausible.
  • Match the method to the data and the design. Independent groups, paired measurements, and non-normal data call for different tools.
  • Stay sceptical of every result, including your own. Try to prove yourself wrong before you decide that you have found something real.
